# Reviewer Credit Policy

## 1. Purpose

This document defines Permea Core's initial reviewer credit policy.

The goal is to recognize useful review work while keeping reviewer credit, acknowledgement, authorship consideration, and validation claims separate.

## 2. Reviewer Credit Principles

Reviewer credit should be:

- proportional to the review contribution
- tied to documented review work where possible
- respectful of reviewer privacy and confidentiality
- separate from automatic authorship
- separate from external peer review
- clear about whether the review was internal project review or broader external review

Reviewer input can materially improve Permea without implying validation or endorsement.

## 3. Credit Types

### Public Acknowledgement

Credit in a README, release note, paper acknowledgement, or project record for useful review feedback.

### GitHub Reviewer Credit

Credit visible through pull request review, issue discussion, commit review, or maintainer notes.

### Release-Note Credit

Credit in release notes when review materially improved release safety, claim discipline, reproducibility, or artifact boundaries.

### Paper Acknowledgement

Credit in a manuscript acknowledgement when review materially improved the paper but does not meet authorship criteria.

### Authorship Consideration

Consideration under `docs/contributors/AUTHORSHIP_POLICY.md` when reviewer input becomes a substantial paper-specific scientific, methodological, analytical, interpretive, or writing contribution with accountability and final approval.

## 4. Reviewer Credit vs Authorship

Reviewer credit and paper authorship are related but not automatic equivalents.

Review may support authorship consideration when it becomes substantial, paper-specific, and accountable.

Routine review, brief comments, typo fixes, high-level advice, or approval of a pull request usually support acknowledgement or GitHub credit rather than authorship.

No reviewer credit category automatically grants maintainer rights, paper authorship, release approval authority, or validation status.

## 5. When Reviewer Input May Support Authorship Consideration

Reviewer input may support authorship consideration when it includes:

- major scientific reframing of a manuscript
- substantive method critique that changes the analysis
- substantial source-to-claim review for core claims
- benchmark interpretation central to the paper
- major reproducibility correction or rerun design
- dataset/provenance work central to evidence interpretation
- significant writing or revision of manuscript sections
- accountability for assigned scientific content
- final approval before submission

Authorship consideration still requires manual approval.

## 6. When Reviewer Input Usually Remains Acknowledgement Only

Reviewer input usually remains acknowledgement or contributor credit when it includes:

- brief comments
- typo or formatting suggestions
- minor wording suggestions
- general encouragement
- informal advice
- limited issue triage
- simple approval without substantive review
- suggestions not incorporated into the paper or release

Acknowledgement can still be valuable and should be recorded where appropriate.

## 7. Anonymous / Confidential Review Option

Some reviewers may request anonymity or confidential handling, especially for early manuscript review, partner-sensitive feedback, or institution-sensitive topics.

When confidentiality is requested:

- do not disclose reviewer identity without permission
- record review status in public-safe terms
- avoid exposing private correspondence
- separate confidential reviewer notes from public release artifacts

Anonymous or confidential review does not imply external peer review unless a formal peer-review process exists and is explicitly described.

## 8. Institutional Reviewer Considerations

University, nonprofit, lab, company, or partner reviewers may have institutional constraints.

Before naming an institutional reviewer or affiliation publicly, confirm:

- permission to acknowledge
- preferred name and affiliation format
- whether the review was personal or institutional
- whether confidentiality, embargo, or partner terms apply
- whether the review can be referenced in public release materials

This policy does not make legal conclusions about institutional policy, employment status, IP ownership, or authorship disputes.

## 9. Credit Dispute Placeholder

Reviewer credit disputes should be handled through documented manual escalation.

Until a fuller dispute process exists, escalate to maintainers and founder/manual approvers.

Avoid public accusations or disclosure of private correspondence.

## 10. Manual Approval Requirement

Manual approval is required before:

- listing reviewer credit in a paper
- converting reviewer credit into authorship
- naming confidential reviewers publicly
- representing review as external review
- representing review as validation or endorsement
- using institutional names in release-sensitive contexts

Reviewer credit should be recorded conservatively when approval status is unclear.

## Claim-Boundary Reminder

Permea may use "AlphaFold for Delivery" as ambition or positioning only.

Permea must not claim AlphaFold-level performance, adoption, or standardization; completed wet-lab validation unless documented; clinical efficacy; universal delivery prediction; production-grade drug delivery platform status; or dataset redistribution permission without source/license approval.

Benchmark claims must remain scoped to dataset, split, metric, model, artifact, and evidence level.

Review does not equal peer review. Reviewer input does not equal external validation. Wet-lab protocol discussion does not equal wet-lab validation.
